## @tylavr

Participating in the Environmental, Social, and Governance reporting course with The ESG Institute has taught me not only about sustainable principles in business, but how we can apply required frameworks in real-world scenarios. This is invaluable in nature conservation, food security, and to communities in educational and occupational outlook. 
A relevant example in South Africa is the case for fracking of non-renewable resources in the Karoo, in discussion because of the supply cliff in Mozambique by 2028. The initiative was halted in South Africa by the Supreme Court of Appeal given safety and health violations in the Environmental Management Act (Act No. 107 of 1998). The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) standards require accurate disclosures to be incorporated in these litigation cases. This may involve collecting data on the planned permits and any chemicals or radioactivity materially impacting health, society, the economy, biodiversity or resources, as done in Strategic Environmental Assessment (SEA) reports or greenhouse emissions investigations in South Africa. 
The GRI and ESG pillars will have an important role in building trust, and protecting the voices and interests of the local communities and environment. The certificate has prepared me with a new perspective to responsible decision-making for a sustainable future in the era of digitalisation and data-driven transformation. How can organisations approach balancing investor or regulator trust, with environmental and social responsibility?
