Participating in the Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) reporting course with The ESG Institute has helped me bridge the gap between my foundational knowledge and experience in the economics industry, and the practical skills required for sustainable practices in business, environmental, and information systems. Sustainability is important in contributing to nature conservation, agribusinesses, food security, people and the global community through goals of positive development, education and employment growth. I expected this course to just be a review of enterprise best practices, but it provided a much deeper dive into sustainability and how we can apply these principles and frameworks in real-world scenarios. For example, a case study includes how fracking for non-renewable resources in the Karoo basin has generated large opposition from energy and environment interest groups, while investment appetite may increase due to the energy supply run out from Mozambique by 2028 (African Energy Leadership Centre, 2025). However, the risky practice was halted in South Africa by a Supreme Court Appeal (RSA, 2019) given safety risks and health violations in accordance with Section 24(5) and 44 of the Environmental Management Act (Act No. 107 of 1998). The Strategic Environmental Assessment (SEA) and various GHG emissions investigations of development scenarios found socioeconomic negative externalities in local populations' health, agriculture, economic job losses from green investing in the energy mix, tourism, social fabric, biodiversity, land resource and water impact in the predicted 17% deficit by 2030, that would need to be accurately and legally disclosed for economic and environment sustainability.
